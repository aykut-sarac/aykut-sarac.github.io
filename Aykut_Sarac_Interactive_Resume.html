Technology & the future
What's accelerating right now, and what it actually means for the next few years
Models are improving faster than most teams can absorb them. The interesting question isn't "what can AI do today" — it's what happens to software roles once the bottleneck moves from typing code to deciding what's worth building.
4 min read
▸ Read full post

Every year in this industry has felt fast. This one feels different. In the space of about two years, I went from using autocomplete that finished a line of code, to working alongside tools like Claude and OpenAI Codex that can hold an entire feature's context, propose an architecture, write the tests, and explain the trade-offs in plain English. That's not an incremental jump — it's a change in what a single developer can be responsible for.

The technology that's moving fastest right now isn't really "AI" as a single thing. It's the combination of three trends landing at once: models that reason over much longer context, agentic tooling that lets those models take multi-step actions instead of just answering questions, and a drop in the cost of running all of this that makes it viable to use constantly rather than occasionally. Put together, that's the difference between "a smart autocomplete" and "a collaborator that can carry a task from a vague description to a working pull request."

What I think this changes, practically, is where the value of a developer sits. Writing syntactically correct code was never really the hard part of the job — understanding the actual problem, the constraints of the business, and the shape of a system that will still make sense in two years was always the hard part. AI tools are compressing the time it takes to go from idea to working code so aggressively that the remaining bottleneck is almost entirely judgment: knowing what to build, what not to build, and when the AI's confident-sounding answer is quietly wrong.

My honest prediction is that the next few years separate developers into two groups, and it won't be about who uses AI tools and who doesn't — almost everyone will. It'll be about who used the time AI saved them to get better at the things AI still can't do: reading a room of stakeholders with conflicting priorities, designing systems that age well, and knowing when "fast" is the wrong goal. The tools are accelerating the easy 80% of the job. The 20% that was always hard is, if anything, becoming more valuable, not less.

AI & the workforce
AI is fast-forwarding skill — and quietly creating gaps we haven't named yet
Using Claude and Codex daily has made me dramatically faster. It has also shown me, in my own work, exactly where the shortcut becomes a liability if you're not paying attention.
5 min read
▸ Read full post

I want to be upfront about something: AI-assisted development has made me better and faster at my job. I use it daily to debug, refactor, explore unfamiliar parts of a codebase, and draft documentation I'd otherwise put off. None of what follows is an argument against using these tools — it's an observation about a side effect I've watched happen, including in my own habits, that I think is worth naming honestly.

The shortcut these tools offer is real: a problem that used to take an hour of digging through documentation and Stack Overflow now takes minutes of conversation. The risk is what happens to the hour you didn't spend. That hour wasn't just "time to find the answer" — it was also the time where you built an internal model of why the answer was the answer. Skip that often enough, and you can end up with someone who can ship a working feature without being able to explain why it works, or confidently debug it when the AI's first suggestion doesn't fit the actual problem.

I've seen the early version of this in myself. There have been moments where I accepted a refactor Codex suggested because it looked clean and passed the tests, then realised later I couldn't have written a clear explanation of the trade-off it made. That's a flag, not a catastrophe — but if it happens often enough, across enough people, it adds up to a workforce that's faster at producing code and slower at actually understanding the systems it's responsible for. That gap doesn't show up in a sprint demo. It shows up eighteen months later, during an outage, when nobody in the room can reason confidently about why the system is behaving the way it is.

The fix isn't "use AI less." It's treating AI-assisted output the way a good senior engineer treats a junior's pull request: useful, often excellent, but not exempt from being understood before it ships. In practice, that means I still make myself explain a non-trivial AI-generated change in my own words before I commit it, and I still deliberately work through some problems the slow way, specifically so the muscle doesn't atrophy. The tool accelerates the work. It can't accelerate the understanding — that part is still on us, and it's the part that's easiest to quietly skip.
